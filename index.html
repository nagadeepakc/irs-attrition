Projecting Attrition at Internal Revenue Service
Tell Me More

Background

The IRS is responsible for collecting taxes and administering the Internal Revenue Code, a complex set of laws affecting virtually every American individual and business. During Fiscal Year (FY) 2016, the IRS collected more than $3.3 trillion in taxes, about 92 percent of all federal revenue. To complete this task, the IRS’s operating expenses come from money annually appropriated from Congress as part of their Annual Appropriations process. Each year, the IRS’s Chief Financial Office (CFO) is responsible for dividing annually appropriated money. In accordance with Federal Appropriations Law, the CFO must allocate money to specific appropriations -- Taxpayer Service, Operations Support and Business Systems Modernization (BSM), and Enforcement. Each Fund is further subdivided into 9-14 Financial Plans. Each Business Unit might have employees that work in multiple Financial Plans. It takes Congressional assistance to transfer funding between Funds, which is difficult to accomplish. Additionally, each Business Unit is reluctant to allow funds to be transferred to a separate Business Unit. This means that the CFO must estimate the funding required by each Business Unit as accurately as possible. Since 2010, the agencies annual budget has been reduced by $900 million and the IRS has lost almost 17,000 full-time employees, while the number of returns has increased to nearly 150 million households. Accurately estimating attrition represents the best way for the IRS to free up funding, invest in unfunded requirements, and align with the IRS’ Strategic Plan and the President’s Management Agenda.

How can the IRS become a more efficient tax administrator?

Accurate labor forecasts

Accurate labor forecasts are important because the IRS has limited ability to transfer funds if their initial annual projections prove inaccurate.

Better attrition modeling

Better attrition modeling would improve the IRS’ effectiveness by enabling them to offset the effect of budget reductions.

Our team analyzed a variety of methods used in the IRS to project attrition. The systems that produce the models, accuracy of the models, and sensitivity of the models were all considered. Accuracy was measured by comparing each projection to historical data. The reliability and subjectivity of the methods were also considered. Ultimately, this paper provides recommendations for improvement that could allow the IRS to redistribute $41 million per year for strategic planning purposes. The paper concludes with future challenges of attrition modeling in the IRS.

Problem

The requirements elicitation process with our project Sponsor resulted in one primary goal with several minor goals.

Compare

Analyze

Recommend

Our primary goal is to compare the various methods used in the IRS to estimate attrition and suggest process improvements. Additional lower priority goals include discovering the average time in the fiscal year that employees in each Fund and Financial Plan leave the IRS, the sensitivity of the attrition rate to various factors, and completing a risk analysis of attrition modeling. The average time in the fiscal year that employees leave the IRS is referred to as the Loss Factor. The methods were elicited through interviews from five IRS business units. The team then analyzed them over a thirteen year period. The findings reveal organizational process improvements that will enable the IRS to redistribute approximately 41 million dollars per year earlier in the year for unfunded requirements.

Approach

Our initial task was to gain access to historical attrition data going back through Fiscal Year 2006. This data enabled us to have a baseline with which to compare other method’s accuracy. The team then conducted 47 interviews over the course of ten weeks with IRS personnel across the divisions to reveal workforce attrition and/or planning models that have been developed. Some business units had existing planning models that varied in their degree of sophistication and long-term forecasting capability. However, there has not historically been a consistent attrition forecasting methodology in place across all business units. Within each business unit, methods are similarly inconsistent from year-to-year. Our team worked with subject matter experts who explained existing models and contributed knowledge to aid in development of a corporate strategic workforce planning tool. The team then applied each model to fiscal years 2006-2017 where possible to deduce the accuracy of each model. The team investigated what makes some models more or less accurate than others. Finally, the team suggested improvements, both in which methods should be used and the overall process, to improve the accuracy of labor funding to the business units.

The various BUs follow one of more of the following:


  • Previous Attrition

    The team uses last year’s attrition data and estimates that it will be the same for the upcoming year.

  • Previous Attrition + 0.5%

    The team uses last year’s attrition data, increases the count by a small buffer (0.5%) and reports that for the upcoming year.

  • Previous Attrition 5-year Average

    The team uses a running average from the last 5 years to estimate the attrition in the upcoming year.

  • Lower of Previous Attrition in past two years

    The team uses the lower of the attrition rates from the last two years as the estimate for the attrition in the upcoming year.

  • Previous Attrition 3-year Average

    The team uses a running average from the last 3 years to estimate the attrition in the upcoming year.

  • Previous Attrition 2-year Average

    The team uses a running average from the last 2 years to estimate the attrition in the upcoming year.

  • % of Year Loss Factor

    Relying on the fact that some attrition projections from some Business Units are not required until most of the fiscal year has already passed, this method uses five-year average historical probability distribution functions to estimate what % of total fiscal year attrition occurs in the remaining pay periods, then estimates total attrition by multiplying current attrition by the resulting factor.


  • RAAS


OPERA Projection (RAAS)

The RAAS Model is the most statistical model, which uses logistic regression to calculate the probability of retirements or resignations at the employee level and rolls these up into the Fund and Functional Area categories required by the CFO. This analysis includes factors based on the type of employee (permanent, seasonal, etc), employee-factors such as veteran status and age, and Economicsnomic factors such as the household sales in the county of employment and the local Woods & Poole Economics Wealth Index. Their forecast includes estimates of attrition projected forward one, three, and five-years.
